The mysterious Indus Valley Civilisation

Ancient South Asia's Forgotten Empire

One of the world's oldest and most enigmatic civilisations flourished along the banks of the Indus River around 3300 BCE. Stretching across what is now Pakistan, northwest India, and parts of Afghanistan, the Indus Valley Civilisation — also known as the Harappan Civilisation — was, at its peak, the most extensive of the ancient world's great cultures, surpassing even Egypt and Mesopotamia in geographic reach.

A Civilisation Ahead of Its Time

What makes the Indus Valley Civilisation so remarkable is the sophistication of its urban planning. Cities like Mohenjo-daro and Harappa were laid out on precise grid systems, with wide main streets and carefully organised residential blocks.

Perhaps most impressively, they possessed advanced drainage and sanitation infrastructure — covered sewers, indoor bathrooms, and public wells — that would not be seen again in the region for thousands of years. These were not rough settlements but mature, well-administered cities with populations potentially reaching tens of thousands.

Top 3 Indus Valley Civilisation facts

  1. The script has never been decoded. We may never know their language.
  2. The Indus Valley Civilisation was bigger than ancient Egypt and Mesopotamia combined.
  3. There is no evidence of warfare.
